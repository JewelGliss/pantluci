What is Tsohci?

Tsohci ([t͡soː.ci]) is an experimental constructed language designed to explore the bounds of what a human-usable language can be by using a breadth-first inspired grammar which appears in few if any natural or constructed languages. To make up for the unfamiliarity, it uses a largely simple grammar that attempts to minimize the number of things a speaker or listener needs to think about. The language was developed beginning in late June 2021 following various sketches of potential breadth-first languages inspired by xkcd 2407. It originally used a modified consonant inventory from unrelated language sketches from December 2020, a vowel inventory copied from Lojban, a tone system copied from Láadan, and a grammar system heavily inspired by toki pona. By early 2022 most of these influences were largely obscured by revisions to the language.

Tsohci is intended to explore many linguistic theories that claim some form of fundamental or underlying genetic language or grammar. This language violates most if not all of their proposed systems, so if it can be learned to fluency then those theories are likely false. However, Tsohci is a hobby project at best and should not be taken seriously enough to be considered actual science. It exists to be interesting and entertaining, so no enforced standard of Tsohci will ever have official support, and speakers will be encouraged to speak how they wish to.

Morphology

Syllables in Tsohci are of the form CV, i.e. consonant followed by vowel that is either long or short. Words may be any combination of syllables with the following restrictions:

  • the final syllable may not be the same as the penultimate syllalbe
  • the final syllable may not contain a long vowel
  • the first syllable may not contain a long stop
  • stress rests on the first syllable
  • The same vowel may not occur on both sides of a glottal stop word-internally.

Allophony

/i/ is realized as [ɪ] after velars.

/ɯ/ is realized as [u] after labials.

/j~ʒ/ is realized as [ʑ] before [j].

Orthography

Tsohci is written using the Latin script with capitalization only on the first letter of every sentence.

The glottal stop is written as a space, and may be elded when between two vowels as long as the first of the two is short or at the start of a word.

Grammar

Syntax

Tsohci grammar is syntactically very simple, relying on only a handful of rules.

  • Sentence breaks always requires exactly two arguments and other words never accept any arguments.
  • To create a sentenc break:
    • if the preceeding word ends in a short vowel, lengthen it
    • if the preceeding word ends in a long vowel, shorten it and reduplicate the final syllable
  • Words are fill the oldest unfilled argument slot.
  • Only certain combinations of word types may legally fill the slots of a sentence break.

while not actually a gramatical rule, the word so exists to insert before the beggining of a text to have something to reduplicate off of.

Syntactic category combinations

Lexical items may be adverbs, conjunctions, nouns, or verbs.

Below are possible combinations of types the arguments of "en" may be, followed by "↦", the type it acts as, ":", and a description of the semantics.

noun, verb ↦ noun: noun with the verb attached as a relative clause. The noun is a typically but not always an experiencer, theme, or patient. This noun is refered to as the subject.

verb noun ↦ verb: verb with the noun incorporated. The noun is a typically but not always an agent, stimulus, or force. This noun is refered to as the object.

(in defintions where the object is unspecified, it can be assumed to be one of these)

conjunction, any type ↦ CP: used in the following rule.

any type, CP (must contain the same type) ↦ the same type: conjoins two of the same types.

verb, adverb ↦ verb: verbs happens in a manner described by the adverb.

adverb, adverb ↦ adverb: 1st adverb is true in a manner described by the 2nd adverb.

Semantics

At the top level of a sentence, as well as within some lexical phrases, the semantics of a clause change somewhat.

Nouns are asserted to either exist, be contextually relavent, or awnser a question.

Verbs are asserted to either happen, be contextually relavent, or awnser a question.

Other clause types are asserted to be a good addition to a previous sentence, or awnser a question.

Vocabulary

Tsohci words are taken from a wide range of languages with the only concerns being whether the word is needed and how much it needs to be modified to obey proper morphology. This means that commonly spoken languages like English, Mandarin Chinese, or Spanish are not given special consideration and are often passed over entirely. The semantic domains of the borrowed word are also mostly carried over with a small level of modification to remove any wholly disconnected polysemy and to simply appease the desires of whoever is loaning the word.

Lexical phrases

Lexical items in Tsohci are not necessarily a single root, and may instead consist of any syntactically valid (ignoring types) construction. These multi word lexical items may also have an equal number of empty spots in their construction and defintions that must be filled when used.

Names and loans

Loan words, including proper nouns, are typically converted to Tsohci's morphology one of two ways:

  • attempting to maximize the recognizability of the pronunciation (the more common method)
  • attempting to maximize the recognizability of the spelling (used only when the spelling of the original word is extremely close to being a valid as a Tsohci word and the pronunciation of the word is not nearly close to being a valid Tsohci word)

Additionally, when using the first method, a few specific recommendations may be followed:

  • vowel height distinctions may be swapped to manipulate the weight of the syllables
  • voice distinctions may be swapped to manipulate the weight of the syllables
  • swapping vowels is preferred to swapping consonants unless the vowel of the word being loaned is a perfect match for a Tsohci vowel.
  • diphthongs typically become dual tones inheriting their first vowel and rising or lowering depending on the vowel weight or hight change.
  • it is preferable to drop a syllable than to gain one unless dropping a syllable would result in a single syllable word
  • low tone may be treated as default with dual tones being used to transcribe vowel length, or high tone to transcribe stress
  • phonemes less common in Tsohci are preferred to more common ones if the word being borrowed has multiple valid pronunciations in the original language
  • unvoiced consonant clusters may become ejectives, and voiced consonant clusters may become implosives

Remeber that sentence breaks requires exactly two arguments and behaves differently depending on the types of those arguments. In the examples above, those types are a noun followed by a verb, and which means the noun with the verb attached as a relative clause. Since the top level of these sentences are noun, the sentences assert that the described noun exist. In other words the first sentence might be more accurately translated as "A big cat exists" but since people dont talk like that in English, a slight less accurate, but pretty much semantically identical translation is listed instead.

Soh mih tetsi citi - A cat likes me

Soh citih tetsi vu - You like a cat

Soh vuh tetsi mi - I like you

Here you can see how a sentence break can be an argument for another sentence break. in these examples the first sentence break functions the same as in previous sentences. While the second one has a verb followed by a noun, and as a result acts as a verb with the noun incorporated. In other words the first sentence might be more accurately translated as "A me that is cat-liked exists", but this is even less like how people speak in English.

Soh mih tetsih citi ni - This cat likes me

Soh vuh tetsih citi suhri - A big cat like you

These are nearly identical to the previous block, but this time the second noun is also experiencing a verb. This is very similar to how adjectives work in english.

Soso suhri citi mi - My cat is big

Soso hunere citi vu - Your cat is red

Soso mı citi ni - This cat is mine

Soso vu citi hunere - A red cat is yours

Soso mı citi suhri - A big cat is mine

Keep in mind that words fill the oldest available argument, so the word order may be different from what you might expect.

Sosoh citi mi tetsi vu - You like my cat

Sosoh citi suhri tetsi mi - I like a big cat

Here you can see the clitic form of a rendered as a prefix of the following word rather than a suffix of the preceeding one, otherwise this is the same as above but with a transitive verb.

Finally, bringing everything together

Sosoh citi hunere tetsih citi suhri - A big cat likes a red cat

All other numbers are made using normal grammar treating numbers 0-99 as verbs, powers of 100 as nouns, and using váa to join digits typically from greatest to least. The noun that the final 0-99 digit is attached to is the thing being counted. negatives are formed by attaching débóo to that unit noun.
